What makes a newscast?

News, at its core, is just information; a newscast is a method of presenting that information to the public. A newscast is usually broken down into segments, each segment relating to an informational story or piece of information(A.K.A, a piece of news).

Analysis of local news station WRAL's 1/9/24 newscast.

Legal and ethical concerns within news production

In order for a newscast to remain impartial, present news objectively, and be true to the viewer, several considerations have to be made. here, I will cover a few.

In the US, filming people without their consent is legal as of the first amendment. News stations have to consider people's privacy for safety, even if it is perfectly legal to disregard.

The cost of production at news stations often runs high, so news stations are rarely ever non-profit; in order to make money, news statons take sponsorships and advertise. Advertisers can threaten to cut funding if the station airs anything that the advertiser doesnt want their name on, which causes the news station to loose some of their transparency.

My response to several ethical dilemmas.

Stages of news production

1. Pre-Production

Pre-Production is a broad catagory that includes processes like outlining the newscast and planning filming and scripts ahead of time, as well as setting deadines, MVPs, and negotiating with clients.

2. Production

Production is the stage where the outine made in the planning stage gets filmed, composited, and edited together.

3. Post-Production

In the Post-Production stage, a newscast gets reviewed, modified, finalized, and aired to the public via radio and satellite television, as well as online.

Project Examples:

Graveside was a community owned skatepark which I spend a lot of my free time at. Unfortunately, it's getting torn down. I thought this would make a really good news story as every skater at athens basically lives there. If you look past the lack of tripods, I think this video turned out very well. I started by coming up with a basic outline of the story and all the different clips I'd need for it. I messaged the main owner to ask for an interview, and the next day I went over to film. After I collected all the clips, made the graphics, and got permission from everyone in the video, I edited it together over the course of two class periods.

Producing a news story about my cheer coach was an interesting experience, one that I learned a lot from nonetheless. I started by asking the team for interview questions, aswell as coming up with some of my own. I set up an interview after practice the next week. While I waited, I thought up a basic outline for how the story would flow, and what clips were needed. The interview went smoothly, I filmed it from a few different angles with different lighting. The other clips I needed was just B-roll of coach working with the team. Being on the team, it was difficult to find time to film her, but I ended up getting just what I needed. I then transcribed all of the interview footage, made a script, recorded voicover, and put it all together. Unfortunately, the sript didn't make it to the final cut due to time restrictions, but it can be seen below.

Athens Ask Example:

"Athens Asks" is a segment where a veriety of students are asked a question, and give their answers to be broadcast to the whole school. It's not meant to be taken seriously, so we are encouraged to have fun with the production. Making these segments are some of the most fun I've had at school, and people like watching them. For this installment, Me and my partner Rylie Burdick asked students what their biggest pet peeves are. We set up a camera at the beginning of class, went out to the lunchroom during "A" lunch, and got some answers. I edited the segment together later that day. No planning, no scripts, just creativity.
